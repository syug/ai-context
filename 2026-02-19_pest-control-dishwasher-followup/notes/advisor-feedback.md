# Strategic Advisor Feedback on Agent Email Draft

## Overall Assessment

The draft is well-structured, polite, and fact-based. It strikes a reasonable balance between asserting rights and maintaining a cooperative relationship with the agent. Below are specific observations and suggested improvements.

---

## 1. Carrot-and-Stick Balance

**Current state:** The "carrot" is present (thanking the agent for previous efforts, noting the property benefit), and the "stick" is deliberately understated. This is appropriate for a first follow-up request.

**Suggestion:** The email could slightly strengthen the legal foundation without becoming threatening. Currently, there is no reference to the landlord's legal obligation. Adding a single, neutral reference to the Residential Tenancies Act 2010 (NSW) would signal awareness of rights without being aggressive. For example, after the pre-existing condition paragraph:

> "Under the Residential Tenancies Act 2010 (NSW), the landlord is obligated to provide and maintain the premises in a reasonable state of repair (s 63), and the property must be reasonably clean and fit for habitation at the start of the tenancy (s 52). As this issue was documented at the commencement of my lease, I believe it falls within the landlord's maintenance obligations."

This is a factual statement, not a threat, and it sets the stage if the matter ever needs to go to NCAT.

---

## 2. NSW Tenancy Law Accuracy

**The draft's implied legal position is sound.** Key points:

- **Section 52** of the Residential Tenancies Act 2010 requires the landlord to provide premises that are reasonably clean and fit for habitation at the start of the tenancy. The ICR evidence (insect carcasses, dirty dishwasher, disagreement with agent's "Clean, Undamaged, Working" assessment) directly supports a claim that this obligation was not met.
- **Section 63** requires the landlord to maintain the premises in a reasonable state of repair during the tenancy. An active cockroach infestation inside a built-in appliance is a repair/maintenance issue.
- The fact that pest control was already arranged at the landlord's cost establishes a precedent -- the agent/landlord already accepted responsibility for the pre-existing pest issue. Requesting follow-up treatment is a continuation of the same issue, not a new claim.

**One caution:** The draft does not explicitly state the legal basis, which is fine for a cooperative first approach. However, if this request is denied, a second communication should reference the specific sections above and mention the option of applying to NCAT for a repair order.

---

## 3. Are We Asking for Too Much or Too Little?

**The request is proportionate and reasonable.** Two items (follow-up pest control + deep cleaning) are directly related to the documented pre-existing condition. This is not an overreach.

**However, consider whether to also mention the dishwasher's mechanical issues.** The ICR documented a broken door catch, warped gasket, and deteriorated dispenser lid. These are separate repair items the landlord should address. Including them here would be strategically risky -- it could dilute the focused pest control request and make the email feel like a complaint list.

**Recommendation:** Keep this email focused on pest control only. Address dishwasher repairs in a separate, subsequent email once the pest issue is resolved. This creates a documented pattern of reasonable, specific requests rather than a single overwhelming demand.

---

## 4. Specific Suggestions for Improvement

### a) Add a soft deadline
The email currently ends with "Please let me know how you would like to proceed" which is open-ended. Adding a gentle timeframe would be more effective:

> "I am happy to coordinate timing for access. Could you please let me know how you would like to proceed within the next week or so?"

This is polite but creates accountability without being demanding.

### b) Mention the health/hygiene angle briefly
Cockroach droppings falling into a dishwasher that washes eating utensils is a health concern. A brief mention strengthens the case:

> "As the dishwasher is used to clean eating utensils, the ongoing presence of cockroach droppings inside the appliance is also a hygiene concern."

### c) Subject line refinement
Current: "Follow-up pest control request -- Unit 6, 5-13 Hutchinson St, Surry Hills"
Suggested: "Unit 6, 5-13 Hutchinson St -- Follow-up pest treatment request (dishwasher area)"

Putting the property address first makes it easier for the agent to file/find, and specifying "dishwasher area" makes it clear this is a targeted request, not a general complaint.

### d) Consider attaching ICR photos
If sending via email, attaching the relevant ICR photos (page 45 dishwasher photos) would strengthen the case. The draft should mention this:

> "I have attached the relevant photos from my condition report for reference."

---

## 5. Risk Assessment

- **Risk of refusal:** Moderate. The agent may push back saying the initial treatment was sufficient. The tenant's documented self-help efforts and the specific dishwasher evidence make a strong counter-argument.
- **Risk of escalation:** Low. The tone is cooperative and the request is reasonable. If refused, the next step would be a formal repair request citing specific legislation, followed by an NCAT application if needed.
- **Risk of damaging relationship:** Low. The email acknowledges previous efforts and frames the request as collaborative.

---

## Summary of Recommended Changes (Priority Order)

1. **Add health/hygiene angle** -- one sentence about droppings in a food-cleaning appliance (high impact, low effort)
2. **Add soft deadline** -- "within the next week or so" (improves actionability)
3. **Add brief legal reference** -- one sentence citing s 52 and s 63 of the RTA (strengthens position without being aggressive)
4. **Refine subject line** -- property address first, specify dishwasher area
5. **Mention ICR photo attachments** -- if sending via email with attachments
6. **Keep dishwasher mechanical repairs for a separate email** -- strategic sequencing
