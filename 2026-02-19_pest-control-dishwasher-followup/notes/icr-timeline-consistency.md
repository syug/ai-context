The dead insects in the fridge vegetable drawer, the grain debris in kitchen drawers, and the carcasses under the sink were pre-existing conditions that the agent either failed to notice or chose not to report. 3. **The agent assessed the dishwasher as "Clean, Undamaged, Working."** The 5 photos submitted by the tenant (p.45) show significant dirt accumulation on the dishwasher floor, shelf, top/roof, and detergent dispenser. The door catch was broken, the right gasket was warped, and the dispenser lid was deteriorated. This assessment was objectively inaccurate.

4. **Pattern of superficial inspection.** The agent's blanket "No" on pests combined with the demonstrably inaccurate dishwasher assessment suggests a cursory inspection that did not extend to checking inside appliances, drawers, or cupboards -- exactly the locations where pest evidence was found.

5. **Legal significance.** Under NSW law, the agent's pre-tenancy inspection is supposed to document the actual condition of the premises. **Conclusion:** The agent's "Pests: No" was **not justified**. The pest evidence (insect carcasses, grain debris) was a pre-existing condition that a reasonably thorough inspection should have identified.

### Connection to current pest issue (2/19-2/20):

1. **Broken door catch + warped gasket = pest entry points.** A properly sealed dishwasher door prevents insects from entering the internal cavity. A broken door catch means the door does not close fully/securely. A warped gasket means the seal is compromised. Together, these create gaps that cockroaches can use to enter and nest inside the dishwasher body.

2. **Missing frame/trim above dishwasher (discovered 2/19).** This is a NEW finding not in the ICR, but it represents an additional structural gap that connects the dishwasher's internal cavity to the wall void behind it -- a classic cockroach highway. This missing trim, combined with the documented mechanical failures, explains why roaches have colonized the dishwasher interior.

3. **Dirt accumulation = evidence of long-standing neglect.** The significant dirt documented in the ICR photos suggests the dishwasher had not been properly cleaned or maintained for an extended period before the tenancy. This is consistent with a pest infestation that predates the current tenant.

4. **Black particles falling from door interior (2/4, 2/20).** The tenant first noticed black particles (cockroach droppings) on 2/4 when running the dishwasher for the first time. This recurred on 2/20. The ICR's documentation of the dishwasher's poor condition at move-in supports the argument that these droppings were already accumulating inside the door structure before the tenant moved in.

5. **Built-in unit limitation.** The ICR photos show the dishwasher is an integrated/built-in unit. The 2/10 pest treatment could not access behind or inside this unit. This is documented in the timeline and supports the request for a follow-up treatment that pulls the unit out.

**Conclusion:** The ICR's dishwasher documentation directly supports the current pest claim. The broken door catch, warped gasket, dirt accumulation, and (subsequently discovered) missing frame/trim collectively explain how cockroaches accessed and nested in the dishwasher's internal structure. The ICR establishes this was a pre-existing condition.

## 6. Potential Weaknesses and Counterarguments Daniel Might Raise

### 6a. "The tenant may have brought the pests"

**Counterpoint:** Insect carcasses were found in the fridge and kitchen drawers within 2 days of move-in (2/5). Dead insect carcasses take time to accumulate. A tenant moving in on 2/3 cannot have caused dead insects to appear in a sealed fridge vegetable drawer by 2/5. The grain debris in drawers was also clearly pre-existing (leftover from the previous tenant/occupant). The ICR's contemporaneous documentation of these findings is strong evidence of pre-existing conditions.

### 6b. "The ICR was submitted on 2/10, the same day as pest treatment -- maybe findings were exaggerated"

**Counterpoint:** The ICR review was started on 2/3 (audit trail confirms initial review at 11:13 on 03/02/2026). The findings were documented progressively over 7 days. The coincidence of the submission date and treatment date actually *strengthens* the tenant's position: it shows that by the time of submission, the pest issue was severe enough to warrant professional treatment, which the landlord agreed to fund.

### 6c. "The dishwasher issues are wear-and-tear, not a pest problem"

**Counterpoint:** The broken door catch and warped gasket are mechanical failures that should have been repaired before the tenancy commenced (s.52). Their role as pest entry points is an additional consequence of the landlord's failure to maintain the appliance. The two issues (mechanical failure and pest infestation) are causally linked.

### 6d. "We already paid for pest control on 2/10 -- the problem should be resolved"

**Counterpoint:** The 2/10 treatment explicitly could not reach the dishwasher interior because it is a built-in unit. The treatment addressed accessible areas only. The ongoing droppings (2/20) prove the infestation inside the dishwasher was not resolved. A follow-up is the natural continuation of the same obligation, not a new request.

### 6e. "The tenant should have reported sooner / the 2/14, 2/17-18 sightings are new infestations"

**Counterpoint:** The 2/14 and 2/17-18 sightings (live cockroaches) are consistent with an ongoing infestation that the initial treatment did not fully resolve. The fact that juvenile cockroaches were found (2/14, 2/17-18) indicates a breeding population, which is consistent with a long-standing infestation rather than a new one. The ICR evidence of dead insects at move-in supports the "pre-existing breeding colony" interpretation.

### 6f. "The tenant's own baiting/sealing may have altered the situation"

**Counterpoint:** The tenant's self-help measures (baits, steel wool, spray) are mitigation efforts that a reasonable tenant would undertake. They do not release the landlord from the obligation to address the root cause. The measures also demonstrate the tenant's good faith in managing the situation while waiting for landlord action. Under s.64 RTA 2010, a tenant may carry out emergency repairs if the landlord fails to act within a reasonable time for urgent repairs (pest infestation affecting health could qualify).
